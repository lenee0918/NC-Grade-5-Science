Physical Science

Matter and its interactions; force, motion, and the relationship between them.

PS.5.1.1
Carry out investigations to compare the weight of objects before and after an interaction.
Big idea: weight is conserved before and after many interactions.

Teacher Planning Notes

Students investigate what happens to weight when materials are combined, separated, or changed physically. Emphasize careful measuring, recording, and comparison rather than memorization.

weightbalancemeasurecompareevidence
Sample connected text: During science class, Mira placed 20 grams of sand in one cup and 30 grams of pebbles in another. Before mixing them, the total weight was 50 grams. After pouring the two materials into one container, the class measured the mixture again. The scale still read 50 grams. Mira explained that the materials had changed location, but the amount of matter had not changed.

Quick Look

  • Best evidence task: weigh parts, combine parts, reweigh.
  • Common confusion: “It looks different, so it must weigh different.”
  • Good extension: compare open vs closed systems when something spills or evaporates.
Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingWhat the Teacher Might Hear
FoundationalRecognizes that objects can be weighed and compared using tools such as a balance or scale.“This one is heavier.” “We can measure it.”
DevelopingMeasures the weight of separate materials before and after combining or rearranging them.“I weighed each part first and then checked the total.”
ProficientExplains with evidence that the total weight remains the same after many interactions when no material is lost.“The pieces changed form or location, but the total weight stayed the same.”
ExtendingAnalyzes situations where measured weight changes and explains that matter may have been lost, added, spilled, or not fully captured.“The weight changed because part of the material was not still in the system.”

Anchor Chart Idea

Part A = 20 g+Part B = 30 g→Total = 50 g

Add a teacher-made balance sketch and sentence stem: The total weight before the interaction was ___, and after the interaction it was ___.

PS.5.1.2
Carry out investigations to explain whether the mixing of two or more substances results in new substances.
Big idea: some mixtures are just combinations, while some interactions produce something new.

Teacher Planning Notes

Students need repeated opportunities to compare simple mixtures with interactions that show evidence of a new substance, such as gas production, color change, temperature change, or a new solid.

Sample connected text: Jordan mixed salt into water and noticed that the salt seemed to disappear, but the liquid still looked like water. Later, he mixed baking soda with vinegar and saw fizzing bubbles form right away. The class discussed whether both interactions were the same. They decided that in the second investigation, the evidence suggested a new substance had formed.

Vocabulary Practice

substancemixtureinteractionevidencenew substance

Strong sentence stem: I think a new substance did / did not form because…

Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingPlanning Moves
FoundationalIdentifies that substances can be mixed and observed.Use side-by-side tubs, jars, and simple observations.
DevelopingDescribes visible evidence from mixing, such as bubbles, color changes, or temperature changes.Prompt students to record exactly what changed.
ProficientUses investigation evidence to explain whether a new substance formed or the materials were simply mixed together.Ask students to defend their claim with evidence.
ExtendingCompares multiple interactions and justifies why some show stronger evidence of new substances than others.Introduce sorting tasks and argument writing.

PS.5.1.3
Carry out investigations to compare how heating and cooling affect some materials and how this relates to their purpose and practical applications.
Big idea: temperature changes can affect materials in ways that matter in real life.

Teacher Planning Notes

Keep the focus on observing, comparing, and connecting changes to use. Students should notice that some materials melt, harden, soften, expand, or change shape when heated or cooled.

Sample connected text: Engineers choose different materials for different jobs. A metal spoon can stay strong in hot soup, while a plastic spoon may bend if it gets too hot. Chocolate softens when heated, but butter melts even faster. By testing materials and noticing how they respond to heat and cold, scientists can decide which material is best for a certain purpose.

Good Planning Questions

  • What changed when the material was heated?
  • What happened again when the material cooled?
  • Why would this make the material useful or not useful?
Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingEvidence of Mastery
FoundationalObserves that heating and cooling can change a material.Notices “it melted,” “it got hard,” or “it softened.”
DevelopingCompares how two or more materials respond differently to heating or cooling.Records side-by-side observations in a chart.
ProficientExplains how a material’s response to temperature affects its practical use.“Metal works well for cooking because it keeps its shape.”
ExtendingEvaluates which material is the best choice for a task based on evidence from temperature investigations.Selects and justifies the strongest option.

PS.5.2.1
Carry out investigations to explain how factors such as gravity, friction, and change in mass affect the motion of objects.
Big idea: motion changes when forces or mass change.

Teacher Planning Notes

Students should test one factor at a time. Ramps, toy cars, textured surfaces, and added weights work well for concrete evidence.

Sample connected text: Ava rolled the same toy car down two ramps. One ramp was smooth, and the other had a rough strip of fabric on it. The car on the smooth ramp traveled farther because there was less friction. Then she added weight to the car and noticed the motion changed again. Ava concluded that gravity, friction, and mass can all affect how an object moves.

Vocabulary Practice

gravityfrictionmassmotionforce

Quick oral prompt: Which factor changed the motion the most, and what is your evidence?

Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingTeacher Moves
FoundationalRecognizes that objects can move faster, slower, farther, or in a different way.Use clear demonstrations and descriptive language.
DevelopingIdentifies gravity, friction, or added mass as variables that can change motion.Use isolated one-variable investigations.
ProficientExplains with evidence how each factor changes motion in an investigation.Require claims tied to measured results.
ExtendingPredicts motion changes in new situations and justifies predictions using force concepts.Add design challenges and transfer tasks.

Force-Effect Anchor Chart

  • More friction: often less distance
  • Less friction: often more distance
  • Gravity: pulls objects downward
  • Change in mass: can change how motion responds

PS.5.2.2
Use mathematics and computational thinking to infer the motion of an object (including position, direction, and speed).
Big idea: numbers, measurements, and data help us describe and predict motion.

Teacher Planning Notes

Use charts, line plots, simple graphs, and repeated timed trials. The goal is not advanced formulas, but making sense of data about position, direction, and speed.

Sample connected text: Every two seconds, Eli marked where a battery-powered car was on the floor with tape. After 2 seconds, the car had moved 20 centimeters. After 4 seconds, it had moved 40 centimeters. After 6 seconds, it had moved 60 centimeters. Eli used the pattern in the table to infer that the car was moving forward at a steady rate.

Useful Teacher Prompts

  • What pattern do you notice in the data?
  • How do you know the object kept moving in the same direction?
  • What does the graph tell you about speed?
Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingSample Product
FoundationalDescribes where an object is and which direction it is moving.Simple oral description or picture.
DevelopingUses a table or number line to record position over time.Completed data chart with labels.
ProficientUses measurements and patterns in data to infer direction, position changes, and speed.Accurate graph explanation or data-based conclusion.
ExtendingCompares motion patterns across objects and explains which object moved faster or changed direction.Written comparison using data as evidence.

Life Science: Human Body Systems

Structures and systems of the human body that perform functions necessary for life.

LS.5.1.1
Use models to recognize the organizational structure of humans as a multicellular organism (cell, tissue, organ, system, organism).
Big idea: the body is organized in levels from smallest working units to the whole organism.

Teacher Planning Notes

Students need to see hierarchy clearly and repeatedly. Physical nesting cards, foldables, and body-system examples support this standard well.

Sample connected text: A single muscle cell can do only one small job, but many muscle cells working together form muscle tissue. Different tissues combine to make an organ, such as the heart. Organs that work together form a body system, such as the circulatory system. All of the systems together make one living human organism.

Vocabulary Practice

celltissueorgansystemorganism

Have students practice saying the sequence forward and backward.

Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingEvidence of Readiness
FoundationalIdentifies that living things are made of smaller parts.Can name at least cells and organs.
DevelopingSequences the levels: cell, tissue, organ, system, organism.Accurate ordering task or foldable.
ProficientUses a model to explain how smaller structures combine to form larger body structures.Can connect a named organ to a system and the whole organism.
ExtendingApplies the organization pattern to specific body examples and explains why this structure is efficient for life functions.Explains with a specific example such as heart tissue → heart → circulatory system.

Hierarchy Diagram

Cell→Tissue→Organ→System→Organism

Anchor Chart Prompt

Example: muscle cell → muscle tissue → heart → circulatory system → human

Ask students to build additional examples.

LS.5.1.2
Use models to compare the major systems of the human body (digestive, respiratory, circulatory, muscular, skeletal, nervous) as it relates to their functions necessary for life.
Big idea: body systems have different jobs, but they work together.

Teacher Planning Notes

Push students beyond naming parts. Students should compare function and notice system interaction: oxygen transport, movement, support, response, and processing nutrients.

Sample connected text: When Sofia runs across the playground, several body systems work together. Her respiratory system brings in oxygen. Her circulatory system carries that oxygen to her muscles. Her muscular and skeletal systems help her move, while her nervous system sends and receives messages so she can react quickly. Her digestive system has already helped provide the nutrients and energy her body needs to keep going.

Compare-Contrast Focus

  • What is each system’s main job?
  • Which systems work together in this scenario?
  • How would the body be affected if one system did not do its job well?
Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingTeacher Evidence
FoundationalNames major body systems and matches them to a basic function.Simple matching activity.
DevelopingDescribes how two systems differ in function.Uses compare-contrast language.
ProficientUses models to compare multiple systems and explain how each supports life functions.Accurate body-systems model explanation.
ExtendingExplains how systems interact in real situations such as exercise, eating, breathing, or reacting to danger.Scenario-based reasoning with multiple systems.

Life Science: Ecosystems

Interdependence of plants and animals within ecosystems.

LS.5.2.1
Engage in argument from evidence to compare the characteristics of several common ecosystems (including estuaries and salt marshes, oceans, lakes and ponds, rivers and streams, forests, and grasslands) in terms of their ability to support a variety of populations.
Big idea: ecosystems differ, and those differences affect what can live there.

Teacher Planning Notes

Students should compare ecosystems using evidence such as water type, plant life, shelter, temperature patterns, and available food sources. This standard works well with photographs, maps, and short texts.

Sample connected text: A salt marsh and a grassland can both support many living things, but they do not support the same populations. A salt marsh has wet, salty conditions that fit crabs, marsh grasses, and many birds. A grassland has open, drier space that supports grasses, insects, and grazing animals. When scientists compare ecosystems, they look at which conditions help different populations survive.

Vocabulary Practice

ecosystempopulationhabitatsalt marshgrasslandevidence
Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingPlanning Moves
FoundationalRecognizes that different ecosystems have different features and living things.Use pictures and simple labels.
DevelopingDescribes characteristics of several ecosystems and identifies populations likely to live there.Use comparison charts with text evidence.
ProficientCompares ecosystems using evidence to explain how well each can support a variety of populations.Require oral or written argument from evidence.
ExtendingEvaluates which ecosystem would best support a given population and justifies the claim using multiple ecosystem characteristics.Add scenario-based ecological reasoning.

LS.5.2.2
Use models to classify organisms within an ecosystem according to the function they serve: producers, consumers, or decomposers.
Big idea: organisms have roles in moving matter and energy through ecosystems.

Teacher Planning Notes

Focus on function, not just memorized labels. Students should explain why an organism is a producer, consumer, or decomposer.

Sample connected text: In a pond ecosystem, algae make their own food using sunlight, so they are producers. A fish that eats insects is a consumer because it gets energy by eating other organisms. Tiny decomposers in the mud break down dead plants and animals. Together, these roles help keep the ecosystem working.

LS.5.2.3
Use models to infer the effects that may result from the interconnected relationships of plants and animals to their ecosystem.
Big idea: ecosystem changes ripple through connected organisms.

Teacher Planning Notes

Students should use food chains, food webs, and habitat scenarios to reason about cause and effect. Keep “interconnected relationships” central.

Sample connected text: In a forest, a long dry period caused many plants to grow poorly. Because there were fewer plants, some rabbits had less food. As rabbit numbers dropped, foxes had a harder time finding prey. Even though the drought began with the plants, the effects spread through the whole ecosystem.

Strong Instructional Prompt

If one part of the ecosystem changes, what might happen next, and why?

Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingProduct Ideas
FoundationalRecognizes that plants and animals depend on one another and on their habitat.Simple picture discussion.
DevelopingUses a basic food chain or ecosystem model to identify likely effects of a change.Cause-and-effect arrows.
ProficientInfers how a change in one part of an ecosystem can affect multiple connected organisms.Written explanation using a model.
ExtendingAnalyzes competing possibilities and predicts short-term and longer-term ecosystem effects.Scenario analysis with justification.

Cause-and-Effect Flow

Less rain→Fewer plants→Fewer rabbits→Less food for foxes

Food Web Note

Anchor chart prompt: One change can have many effects because ecosystems are connected.

Life Science: Traits and Behaviors

Inherited and acquired traits; instincts and learned behaviors.

LS.5.3.1
Ask questions to compare instincts and learned behaviors.
Big idea: some behaviors are present without teaching, while others are learned from experience.

Teacher Planning Notes

Use animal examples and simple human examples carefully. Students should compare behavior type and explain how they know.

Sample connected text: A spider does not need to attend a lesson to learn how to spin a web. That behavior is an instinct. A puppy, however, may need training to sit when told. That is a learned behavior. Scientists compare behaviors by asking whether the action happens naturally at birth or develops through teaching and experience.

LS.5.3.2
Ask questions to compare inherited and acquired traits.
Big idea: some characteristics are passed from parents, while others develop through life experiences.

Teacher Planning Notes

Keep examples concrete and age-appropriate. Focus on comparing types of traits rather than genetics vocabulary beyond the standard.

Sample connected text: A child may have naturally curly hair like a parent, which is an inherited trait. The same child may also have calluses on their hands from practicing on the monkey bars every day, which is an acquired trait. Inherited traits are passed from parents to offspring, while acquired traits develop because of actions, environment, or experience.

Earth and Space Science

Earth systems, weather, climate, and the water cycle.

ESS.5.1.1
Analyze and interpret data to compare daily and seasonal changes in weather conditions (including wind speed and direction, precipitation, and temperature) and patterns.
Big idea: weather data shows patterns across time.

Teacher Planning Notes

Students need access to tables, graphs, and repeated observations. The standard is about pattern-finding, not isolated weather facts.

Sample connected text: During one week in October, students recorded temperatures each day and noticed cool mornings and warmer afternoons. After several months of data, they saw a bigger pattern: temperatures were generally lower in winter than in late summer. They also compared windy and calm days and tracked rainfall totals. By analyzing the data, the class could compare both daily weather changes and seasonal weather patterns.

Vocabulary Practice

temperatureprecipitationwind speedwind directionpattern

FoundationalReads simple weather measurements and observations.Table reading / matching task.
DevelopingCompares data from different days or seasons.Short-answer comparison.
ProficientAnalyzes and interprets weather data to identify daily and seasonal patterns.Graph interpretation with explanation.
ExtendingCombines multiple data sources to explain richer weather patterns and exceptions.Multi-graph synthesis.

ESS.5.1.2
Analyze and interpret weather data to explain current and upcoming weather conditions (including severe weather such as hurricanes and tornadoes) in a given location.
Big idea: weather data helps explain what is happening now and what may happen next.

Teacher Planning Notes

Students should use authentic weather maps, forecasts, radar images, and data tables. Severe weather examples should stay grounded in interpreting data, not fear-based discussion.

Sample connected text: A class studied a weather map showing dark clouds, falling air pressure, and strong winds moving toward the coast. Radar images showed heavy rain bands getting closer. Based on the data, students explained that stormy weather was already developing and that stronger wind and rainfall were likely next. They also used safety knowledge to discuss what people should do when severe weather is expected.

Useful Teacher Prompt

What evidence from the weather data supports your explanation of what is happening now and what may happen next?

Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingSuggested Supports
FoundationalReads basic weather information such as temperature, clouds, or precipitation.Labeled maps and guided chart reading.
DevelopingUses one data source to describe current weather or make a simple forecast.Single-source forecast talk.
ProficientAnalyzes and interprets weather data to explain current and upcoming conditions in a location.Data-based written explanation.
ExtendingIntegrates several weather data sources and explains how they work together to strengthen a forecast, including severe weather situations.Radar + map + data synthesis.

ESS.5.1.3
Construct an explanation to summarize the ocean’s influences on weather and climate in North Carolina.
Big idea: nearby ocean water strongly affects North Carolina weather and climate.

Teacher Planning Notes

Use maps of North Carolina, coastal vs inland comparisons, and discussions of the Gulf Stream and ocean influence on temperature and moisture. Keep the standard rooted in North Carolina.

Sample connected text: The Atlantic Ocean affects weather and climate across North Carolina. Coastal areas often have more moisture in the air and can experience storms moving in from the ocean. Ocean water can also influence temperatures, keeping some coastal places warmer in winter and cooler in summer than inland areas. Because North Carolina is close to the ocean, the ocean is an important part of the state’s weather and climate patterns.

ESS.5.1.4
Use models to explain how the sun’s energy drives the processes of the water cycle (including evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation).
Big idea: the sun provides energy that keeps water moving through the cycle.

Teacher Planning Notes

Students need a causal explanation, not just a labeled cycle. The sun’s energy should be central in every model and explanation.

Sample connected text: The water cycle keeps water moving through Earth’s systems. The sun heats water on Earth’s surface, causing evaporation. Plants also release water vapor into the air through transpiration. As water vapor cools, it condenses into tiny droplets that form clouds. When enough water collects in the clouds, precipitation falls back to Earth. Without the sun’s energy, this cycle would not keep moving.

Vocabulary Practice

sun’s energyevaporationtranspirationcondensationprecipitationwater cycle
Progression LevelStudent UnderstandingInstructional Checkpoint
FoundationalIdentifies parts of the water cycle in a model.Can label stages correctly.
DevelopingDescribes what happens during evaporation, transpiration, condensation, and precipitation.Can explain each process in words.
ProficientUses a model to explain how the sun’s energy drives the water cycle processes.Connects the sun directly to cycle movement.
ExtendingExplains how changes in heat, weather, or plant presence could affect parts of the cycle.Applies the model to new situations.
